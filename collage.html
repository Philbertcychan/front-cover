Philbert Chan / Things that move me

Collage

Things that move me.

Not curated for anyone in particular. This is what I think about, return to, and aim at.

Space — The scale of things

Pale Blue Dot — NASA/JPL, Voyager 1, Feb 14 1990. Earth from 6 billion km. Public domain.
Hubble Ultra Deep Field — NASA/ESA. ~10,000 galaxies in a patch of sky the width of a dime at arm's length. Public domain.
The Blue Marble — NASA, Apollo 17, Dec 7 1972. Public domain.
Pillars of Creation — NASA/ESA/Hubble Heritage Team, 2014. Public domain.
Falcon 9 first stage landing, CRS-9 — SpaceX/NASA. Public domain.
Full Moon — Gregory H. Revera, CC BY-SA 3.0.
Crab Nebula — NASA/ESA/Hubble. Remnant of a supernova observed by Chinese astronomers in 1054 AD. Public domain.
Hubble Deep Field — NASA/R. Williams (STScI), 1995. The image that changed cosmology. Public domain.
Hubble Space Telescope — NASA. Public domain.
Moonset from the ISS — NASA. Public domain.
Mimas — NASA/JPL/Cassini. Saturn's moon that looks like the Death Star. Public domain.
Parkes Radio Telescope — CSIRO, CC BY 3.0. One of humanity's ears pointed at the sky.

Science — Where things got built

Manhattan from above — CC BY 2.0. The density of human ambition in one frame.
Henry Ford, 1919 — public domain. He didn't invent the car. He invented the production system that made it real.
Albert Einstein — Oren Jack Turner, 1947. Public domain.
Alan Turing at 16. Broke the Enigma. Invented modern computing. The state chemically castrated him for being gay. Public domain.
Richard Feynman — Nobel Foundation, 1965. The person who made physics feel like joy. Public domain.
Trinity Test, July 16 1945 — U.S. Army, public domain. The moment everything changed. Oppenheimer quoted the Bhagavad Gita.
Wright Brothers, Kitty Hawk, Dec 17 1903 — public domain. 12 seconds. 37 metres. Everything after that was different.
ENIAC — U.S. Army, 1946. Public domain. The first general-purpose electronic computer. 18,000 vacuum tubes.
Nikola Tesla, Colorado Springs, 1899 — public domain. A double exposure. He designed the image himself.
Solvay Conference, 1927 — public domain. Bohr, Einstein, Curie, Heisenberg, Dirac, Pauli, Planck, Schrödinger. 17 of the 29 people in this photo were Nobel laureates.
Bunsen & Kirchhoff — public domain. They invented spectroscopy. We now use it to identify stars billions of light years away.
Carpenter ant, macro — Muhammad Mahdi Karim, CC BY-SA 4.0. The complexity in things too small to see.

The universe — At different scales

Golden Gate Bridge — Rich Niewiroski Jr., CC BY 2.5. Engineering as art.
Mona Lisa — Leonardo da Vinci, c.1503–1519. Public domain. The most visited painting in the world.
Starry Night Over the Rhône — Van Gogh, 1888. Public domain. He painted the sky like he knew something about it.
The Starry Night — Van Gogh, 1889. Public domain. Painted from his room in the asylum at Saint-Rémy.
The Creation of Adam — Michelangelo, c.1512. Public domain. Sistine Chapel ceiling.
Colosseum, Rome — Diliff, CC BY-SA 2.5. Built 70–80 AD. Still standing. Civilization leaves marks.
Pyramids of Giza — Ricardo Liberato, CC BY-SA 2.0. Built c.2560 BC. Still standing. We barely understand how.
Empire State Building — public domain. Built in 410 days during the Great Depression. 3,400 workers. 7 million hours.
Fibonacci Spiral — public domain. The same pattern in sunflowers, shells, galaxies.
Mandelbrot Set — public domain. Infinite complexity from a simple equation. You can zoom forever.

Nature — The original engineer

Public domain.
Sunflower — CC BY-SA 2.5. The Fibonacci sequence made visible.
Great White Shark — Pterantula, CC BY-SA 3.0. 400 million years of evolution. Effectively unchanged.
Nelson Lakes, New Zealand — Pseudopanax, CC BY-SA 3.0.
Aurora Borealis from space — NASA, public domain. Solar wind hitting Earth's magnetic field.
Lightning — U.S. Air Force, public domain. A billion volts. A few milliseconds. Visible from 160 km away.
Snowflake — Kenneth Libbrecht/CalTech, CC BY-SA 3.0. No two alike. Every one a hexagon. Physics as art.

Films, manga, music, books

Film · 2014
Interstellar
Christopher Nolan. The closest I've come to awe in a theatre. The ending still bothers me — which is probably the point.
↗ IMDB
Film · 2015
The Martian
The competence fantasy at its best. One problem at a time. No panic. A better operating manual for high-stakes situations than most actual manuals.
↗ IMDB
Anime · 2022
Cyberpunk: Edgerunners
Studio Trigger. The most melancholic and euphoric thing I've watched in recent memory. The ending is correct and I didn't want it to be.
↗ Netflix
Manga · 1998–2003
BLAME!
Tsutomu Nihei. A lone figure moving through an incomprehensibly vast dying megastructure. Almost no dialogue. One of the most original things I've encountered.
↗ VIZ Media
Film · 2014
Whiplash
About what you're willing to give up to become exceptional. Whether he won or lost at the end is the wrong question.
↗ IMDB
Film · 2010
Inception
Nolan again. The question at the end isn't whether the top falls. It's whether it matters. The answer is no. He's already home.
↗ IMDB
Album · 2015
Currents — Tame Impala
Kevin Parker made this alone. Every instrument. The loneliness and the euphoria are the same thing in this record.
↗ Apple Music
Album · 1973
The Dark Side of the Moon
Pink Floyd. Time. Money. Brain Damage. The great mid-century anxiety put to music. Still sounds like the inside of a mind that knows something is wrong.
↗ Apple Music
Album · 1977
Heroes — David Bowie
The title track. Recorded with three microphones at different distances, gated by volume. He wanted the vocal to sound pulled from far away. It does.
↗ Apple Music
Album · 1959
Kind of Blue — Miles Davis
The best-selling jazz album ever made. Recorded in two sessions with almost no rehearsal. Spontaneous and inevitable at the same time.
↗ Apple Music
Book · 2012
The Idea Factory — Jon Gertner
The history of Bell Labs. The transistor, laser, Unix, information theory — all from one place. This is the north star.
↗ Goodreads
Book · 2014
Zero to One — Peter Thiel
The monopoly chapter alone is worth it. Build something new, not a better version of something that already exists.
↗ Goodreads
Book · 1994
Pale Blue Dot — Carl Sagan
"That's here. That's home. That's us." The most important paragraph written about what we are and what we should do about it.
↗ Goodreads
Framework
How to Get Rich — Naval Ravikant
Specific knowledge, leverage, accountability. The cleanest framework I've found for thinking about what to work on and why.
↗ nav.al
Book · 2012
Antifragile — Nassim Taleb
Some things break under stress. Some resist it. Some get stronger from it. The last category is what you want to build and what you want to be.
↗ Goodreads
Book · 1985
Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!
Curiosity as a way of life. He picked locks at Los Alamos because he was bored. He won the Nobel Prize because he couldn't stop wondering why.
↗ Goodreads
